Hybrid Architectures in NLP: A Comparative Survey

This survey presents a systematic comparison of 47 hybrid architectures combining attention and convolution for NLP.
We find that neither pure attention nor pure convolution is universally superior across all task types and domains.
Our analysis shows that task-specific inductive biases determine the optimal architecture choice in most cases.
Results from 47 benchmarks indicate that hybrid models outperform single-paradigm models by an average of 3.2% on standard NLP evaluations.
We demonstrate that computational cost is the primary bottleneck in real-world deployment scenarios.
The survey identifies several open questions including optimal mixing ratios, training strategies, and robustness under distribution shift.
We observe that pre-training objectives significantly interact with architectural choices in ways that are not yet well understood.
Future work should explore dynamic architecture selection mechanisms based on input characteristics and downstream task requirements.
We propose that the community adopt unified benchmarking protocols to enable fair cross-architecture comparisons.
Our analysis reveals that hybrid models are underexplored in low-resource and multilingual settings.
